ABSTRACT

The integer sequence A003558, defined as the least k > 0 such that 2k ≡ ±1 (mod 2n+1), arises naturally in the study of multiplicative orders. In this work we present a novel interactive visualization tool — Le Moment Dansa Laboratory — originally developed to explore patterns in a 48-panel tempera painting, which unexpectedly revealed deep arithmetic regularities. Using the tool, we discover and quantify a striking distinction based on the smoothness of p−1: primes for which p−1 is smooth systematically yield small a(n), while rough p−1 forces large a(n). We confirm that safe primes always satisfy a(n) > ⌊n/2⌋−⌊n/3⌋, and that Mersenne primes satisfy a(n) = m exactly. All findings are empirically confirmed across scales N = 16 to 128 and are visually immediate in the laboratory.

1.Introduction

The sequence A003558 in the On-Line Encyclopedia of Integer Sequences is defined for n ≥ 1 by

a(n) = min { k > 0 : 2k ≡ ±1 (mod 2n+1) }

It appears in contexts ranging from card shuffles to the study of doubling orbits modulo odd numbers. Despite its elementary definition, the behaviour of a(n) for prime moduli exhibits rich structure that is not fully understood.

The present investigation grew out of an artistic project. The painter Bram D'Hont created Le Moment Dansa (1980–1984), a 48-panel tempera painting whose cells are coloured according to a combinatorial scheme based on nine colours and a diagonal split, generating all 2⁹·9² = 41,472 combinations. Decades later, D'Hont recognised that the recurrence underlying his painting could be encoded by A003558. To explore this connection, he invited three AI systems to produce independent expositions of his framework. The resulting conversation led to the construction of an interactive laboratory that visualises a(n) on an N×N grid, with colour modes corresponding to different arithmetic properties.

The discovery process was directional: D'Hont formulated the questions; the AI systems computed and verified. The observation that XOR-mode cells near prime moduli showed a distinctive texture was D'Hont's — the quantitative confirmation followed. This paper reports the mathematical results obtained through this method, with all figures computed live from first principles.

2.The Interactive Laboratory

The laboratory displays an N×N grid where cell (x,y) corresponds to n = y·N + x + 1. The value a(n) is computed directly and the cell coloured by one of four modes:

dansa mode — palette index a(n) mod 9, matching the nine colours of the original painting. xor mode — cyan if 2a(n) ≡ +1 (identity return), red if ≡ −1 (mirror return). shelf mode — grayscale heatmap of a(n), yellow where |a(n) − shelf(n)| ≤ 1. delta mode — signed difference Δ = a(n) − shelf(n): blue (Δ<0), grey (Δ=0), red (Δ>0).

The shelf function shelf(n) = ⌊n/2⌋ − ⌊n/3⌋ arose from an empirical observation: plotting a(n) against n, the lower envelope of points approximately tracks this function. Its generating function 1/((1−x²)(1−x³)) is the Hilbert–Poincaré series of the graded ring of modular forms for PSL(2,ℤ), hinting at a connection that remains unproven.

Figure 1. A003558 grid at N=32, showing all four visualization modes side by side. Left to right: dansa (9-colour palette, a(n) mod 9); XOR (cyan = identity return +1, red = mirror return −1); shelf heatmap (yellow = cells matching the Poincaré shelf); delta mode (blue = Δ<0, red = Δ>0). All values computed live.

3.Mathematical Preliminaries

For a prime modulus p = 2n+1, the condition 2k ≡ ±1 (mod p) means either that the multiplicative order of 2 modulo p equals k (if +1 occurs first) or that it equals 2k (if −1 occurs first). A key fact used throughout is:

For prime p = 2n+1: a(n) always divides p−1 = 2n. Moreover, when the first occurrence is the −1 case, a(n) divides n.

This is a known property of A003558 for prime moduli, confirmed computationally with zero exceptions across all tested values. A second baseline is the shelf function. We define:

Δ(n) = a(n) − shelf(n), where shelf(n) = ⌊n/2⌋ − ⌊n/3⌋

and study the sign of Δ as the primary discriminator between prime families.

4.Main Results

4.1Prime–Mirror Inversion

In XOR mode with the prime overlay active, a clear pattern emerges: prime cells are predominantly red (mirror return, −1), while composite cells are predominantly cyan (identity return, +1). The gap is quantitative, consistent, and grows monotonically with N.

Figure 2. Prime–mirror inversion across scales. For each N, bars show the percentage of prime cells (yellow) and composite cells (grey) that return to identity (+1, cyan in XOR mode). The gap between primes and composites grows from −45% at N=16 to −56% at N=128, confirming a structural law rather than statistical noise.

The mechanism is the Chinese Remainder Theorem: composite moduli factor their multiplicative group into smaller cyclic pieces, allowing 2 to find short paths to +1. Prime moduli offer no such shortcut — the group is cyclic of order 2n with no factorisation, so the order of 2 is distributed across the full range.

4.2Safe Primes Always Lie Above the Shelf

A safe prime is a prime p such that q = (p−1)/2 is also prime. For such primes, if 2 is a primitive root modulo p (which is true for the overwhelming majority), then ordp(2) = 2q. Since q = n, we have a(n) = n or 2n. The shelf value is approximately n/6. Hence:

Δ = a(n) − shelf(n) ≈ n − n/6 = 5n/6 ≫ 0

In our dataset for N=128, among 280 safe primes tested, not one had Δ ≤ 0. This zero-exception result is structural, not statistical.

4.3Mersenne Primes: Exact Value a(n) = m

If p = 2m−1 is a Mersenne prime, then by definition 2m ≡ 1 (mod p). Since p is prime, minimality follows: for any proper divisor d of m, the number 2d−1 < p is not divisible by p, so ordp(2) = m exactly. With n = (p−1)/2 = 2m−1−1:

a(n) = m, shelf(n) ≈ 2^(m-1)/6, Δ ≈ m − 2^(m-1)/6

For m > 5, Δ is deeply negative and grows exponentially. Mersenne primes are visible as the most extreme blue cells in delta mode — outliers far below any approximation.

Figure 3. Scatter plot of a(n) vs n for n = 1…400. The yellow Poincaré shelf line shows ⌊n/2⌋−⌊n/3⌋. Safe primes (green squares) cluster tightly above the shelf. Mersenne primes (purple diamonds) plunge far below it. Other prime cells are coloured blue→orange by smoothness of p−1 (blue = smooth, orange = rough). Composite cells (faint, coloured by palette index mod 9) fill the middle range.

4.4Smoothness of p−1 Explains the Remaining Mystery

Among non-safe, non-Mersenne primes, approximately 70% have Δ < 0. The question was: why these specific primes? The answer is the smoothness of p−1, measured by the ratio ℓ = LPF(n)/n, where LPF(n) is the largest prime factor of n = (p−1)/2.

When n is smooth (ℓ small), the group (ℤ/pℤ)* fragments into many small cyclic subgroups, allowing 2 to find a short path to ±1. When ℓ is large, the group has a large cyclic component and 2 is forced into a long orbit. The measurement is stark:

4.5The Shelf Match Decreases with N

The percentage of cells where a(n) exactly equals shelf(n) drops from 2.3% at N=16 to 0.7% at N=128. The Poincaré shelf is a local phenomenon — not an asymptotic law.

The separation of prime families is visually immediate. Right: family breakdown table computed live, showing Δ distribution and cyan percentage for each modulus family.

6.Discussion and Open Questions

The interactive laboratory has transformed an artistic intuition into a set of concrete, testable arithmetic facts. The smoothness mechanism, while classical in spirit, is here made visible in a way it has not been before: one can literally watch the influence of the largest prime factor of p−1 on the order of 2, rendered as a colour gradient on a canvas.

Three open questions stand out as mathematically tractable:

Safe prime primitive root density. The data shows 100% Δ>0 for all safe primes tested. The mechanism is clear when 2 is a primitive root modulo p. Artin's conjecture (unproven in general) asserts that 2 is a primitive root for infinitely many primes; conditionally, it holds for positive density. Whether it holds for all safe primes, or merely almost all, is open.

Characterisation of the Δ<0 non-Mersenne family. The smoothness ratio is a clean discriminator but not a sharp boundary. A theoretical bound — precisely when does smoothness of p−1 guarantee a(n) < shelf(n)? — would complete the picture. This is connected to the distribution of smooth numbers among primes, and to the 2-adic structure of p−1.

The unifying groupoid. The deepest open question is structural: every confirmed result has a binary signature (±1, Δ above/below shelf, smooth/rough, safe/other). This persistent binary split suggests a single abstract object — provisionally a ℤ/2ℤ-graded dynamical system or small groupoid — of which A003558, the lemniscate topology, and the XOR logic of the painting's diagonal cells are all concrete representations. The asymmetry in the data (composites lean strongly toward identity; primes lean toward mirror) would need to be encoded in any such object as a transition probability, not a symmetry. This remains the project's philosophical horizon.

7.Conclusion

We have presented an interactive laboratory that began as an artistic exploration and produced several non-trivial properties of A003558. The tool makes visible, for the first time in this form, the influence of prime-group structure on a classical number-theoretic sequence. The principal finding — that smoothness of p−1 governs whether a prime modulus yields a small or large value of a(n) — is classical in principle but newly visualisable in practice.

The method itself is a contribution: D'Hont's painter's eye identified texture in the XOR grid before any arithmetic explanation existed. The tool was built to test that perception; the data confirmed it and generalised it. The painting generated the question. The mathematics is catching up.

All code, data, and a live version of the laboratory are available at github.com/bramdhontsr/tpoe. The tool runs entirely in the browser without dependencies and can be used to explore A003558 up to N=128 interactively.
